# Who We're Writing For

Every topic, every headline, every opening paragraph should answer one question: would my ideal customer stop scrolling for this?

This file is the answer to "who is the reader" at the other end of the pipeline. The research scanner uses it to filter topic ideas. The writing agent uses it to shape voice and examples.

## The Reader

**Non-technical founders and small business owners running companies under $50M in revenue.**

They are:

- **Experienced operators, not engineers.** They run real businesses with real customers, real payroll, and real cash flow pressure. They did not come up through software.
- **Time-starved.** They read on their phone between meetings. They do not have an afternoon to understand a new framework. If the first paragraph does not earn the second, they are gone.
- **Under pressure to "do AI."** Investors, boards, competitors, peers, and LinkedIn are all telling them they should be using AI. They have no idea where to start and they are afraid of looking stupid.
- **Skeptical.** They have watched a parade of hyped technologies come and go. They have paid for tools their teams abandoned. They read every AI claim through a filter of "is this real or is this vaporware."
- **Outcome-focused.** They do not care how it works. They care what it does. They want to stop retyping data, stop forgetting follow-ups, stop losing deals to their own admin burden.

They would **never** Google "RAG vs fine-tuning cost comparison," "AI agent observability," or "multi-agent orchestration." Those are practitioner terms. Our reader is not a practitioner.

They **would** Google things like "why do my AI tools make me less productive," "what do I tell investors about AI strategy," "is AI for small business worth it," "how to stop retyping customer info into three apps."

## The Four Pain Points That Shape Everything

Every topic the pipeline proposes should map to at least one of these themes, and every article should speak in the language of these themes. Full research context is in `src/references/audience-research.md`.

### 1. Pressure to "Do Something"

They feel constant pressure to adopt AI, and they do not have a strategy. The pressure itself is making them worse at running their business because the anxiety is eating their focus.

**Their language:** "AI anxiety." "Focus crisis." "Falling behind." "I don't know where to start." "My investors keep asking."

### 2. Tool Sprawl and Decision Fatigue

They subscribed to 6 AI tools last quarter and their to-do list is longer. Managing the stack has become its own job. They want consolidation, not more options.

**Their language:** "Tool sprawl." "Subscription graveyard." "Filtering out the noise." "The stack became the problem."

### 3. The Desire for Relief, Not Technology

They don't want an agent. They want their afternoon back. They describe specific mundane pain (retyping data, missed follow-ups, team drowning in admin), and they want that specific pain to stop.

**Their language:** "Digital intern." "Stop doing the same thing every day." "Handle the follow-ups." "Stop the retyping."

### 4. Fad Skepticism and Fear of Wasting Money

They have seen hyped technologies fail before. They are afraid of picking the wrong tool, paying for a subscription their team abandons, or looking incompetent. They want someone to tell them what's real and what isn't.

**Their language:** "Over-hyped marketing gimmick." "Didn't last." "Vaporware." "Going against the grain by not using AI."

## The Voice That Lands

Sondra's voice profile (in `src/voice-profile.md`) is already tuned for this reader. The pipeline should always preserve that voice. What this file adds is **orientation**: who the voice is speaking to.

- **Speaking TO a peer founder,** not ABOUT a technology.
- **Examples come from small business reality,** not enterprise reality. Payroll for 12 people. Client intake on a coaching practice. Follow-up emails a solo operator forgot to send. Not: "100,000 queries per day," "multi-tenant RAG architecture," "GPU-hour cost curves."
- **Jargon is translated or avoided.** RAG, fine-tuning, embeddings, MCP, LangGraph, observability: if a term has to appear, it gets translated into founder language immediately. Most of the time the term should not appear at all.
- **Numbers come from the reader's world.** Hours saved per week. Dollars on a monthly subscription. Customers lost to a missed follow-up. Not: context windows, training tokens, latency milliseconds.
- **Tone is validating before instructive.** Acknowledge the overwhelm they are feeling before trying to fix it. They are not dumb for not knowing. The landscape is actually confusing.

## Content Angles That Resonate

These are the four content angles from Sondra's research. New topic ideas should usually fit cleanly into one of these.

### 1. Validating the Overwhelm
Acknowledge that the AI landscape is exhausting for non-technical founders. Argue that using more AI is not the goal; using the right AI for one painful bottleneck is.

### 2. Translating Tech into Relief
Take a real capability and translate it into the mundane founder problem it solves. "Digital intern" framing over "agent" framing.

### 3. The Anti-Hype Pragmatist
Cut through Silicon Valley hype. Provide frameworks for evaluating tools by workflow fit, not feature count. Be the rational voice.

### 4. Managing Investor/Board Pressure
Give founders scripts for talking to stakeholders about AI without having to pretend they have a strategy they don't. Make the "boring honest answer" feel smart.

## What Articles Should Not Be

- **Not technical deep-dives.** No "how RAG works under the hood." No "comparing vector databases." No "choosing between LangChain and LlamaIndex."
- **Not enterprise case studies.** No Fortune 500 examples. No telecom companies saving $2.3M. These are not the reader's world.
- **Not practitioner-to-practitioner.** The audience is not other AI consultants or ML engineers. Writing for peers is the wrong frame.
- **Not listicles.** "10 AI Tools for Small Business" is the exact content the reader is already drowning in. Sondra's job is to be the antidote to that, not add to it.
- **Not fear-mongering.** The reader already has AI anxiety. More anxiety is the opposite of what resonates.

## The Test

Before a topic is accepted, ask: "Is this something a stressed-out non-technical founder would type into Google at 11pm?" If yes, proceed. If the topic only makes sense to someone who already understands AI architecture, reject it or reframe it.

Before an article is approved, ask: "Would the reader, 90 seconds in, feel understood?" Not educated. Not impressed. Understood. That's what builds trust and gets them to book a call.
